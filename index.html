Mission

Making mining health risks understandable before symptoms become serious.

MineWatch Ghana is built for workers, families, students, health educators, NGOs, and policymakers who need clear information in ordinary language. Many families only recognize danger when coughing, breathlessness, chest pain, fever, or weakness has already become serious.

The platform explains common mining-related risks, helps users reflect on symptoms and exposure, and visualizes educational risk categories across selected Ghanaian mining areas. It is a bridge between personal experience and public protection.

The Problem

Mining health danger often grows quietly.

Workers and families may see dust, smoke, chemicals, or illness every day without receiving practical health information early enough.

Dust inhalation

Fine dust can enter the lungs during digging, crushing, blasting, hauling, or processing.

Silica exposure

Rock dust containing silica can cause long-term lung scarring after repeated exposure.

Mercury and chemicals

Some mining processes use mercury or other chemicals that can harm the lungs, brain, skin, and family spaces.

Delayed symptoms

Coughing, chest pain, and breathlessness may appear slowly, making people wait too long before seeking care.

Limited information

Health guidance is often written for experts, not for workers, families, students, and local communities.

Families affected

When a worker becomes sick, the whole household can face financial stress, caregiving pressure, and grief.

Health Risks

Simple explanations for common mining-related health risks.

Dust inhalation

What it means
Breathing dust from rock, soil, roads, or processing areas.
Why it matters
Dust can irritate the airways and make breathing problems worse over time.
Common warning signs
Persistent cough, wheezing, chest tightness, and shortness of breath.
What to do early
Reduce dust exposure, use appropriate respiratory protection, and seek screening if symptoms continue.

Silica and pneumoconiosis

What it means
Repeated breathing of fine silica dust from certain rocks can scar the lungs.
Why it matters
Lung scarring can become permanent and may cause serious breathlessness.
Common warning signs
Long-term cough, breathlessness during work, chest pain, and tiredness.
What to do early
Ask for medical screening, avoid dusty tasks where possible, and improve ventilation and wet methods.

Mercury exposure

What it means
Touching mercury or breathing mercury vapor during gold processing or burning.
Why it matters
Mercury can affect the nervous system, kidneys, lungs, pregnancy, and children.
Common warning signs
Headache, tremors, weakness, breathing irritation, mood changes, and memory problems.
What to do early
Avoid direct contact, do not burn mercury near homes, and seek health advice after exposure.

Chemical exposure

What it means
Contact with processing chemicals, fuel, acids, solvents, or contaminated water and soil.
Why it matters
Chemicals can harm the skin, eyes, lungs, stomach, and long-term health.
Common warning signs
Burning eyes, skin irritation, dizziness, nausea, cough, or unusual weakness.
What to do early
Wash exposed skin, move away from fumes, keep chemicals away from children, and seek care for symptoms.

Pneumonia and respiratory infections

What it means
Infections that affect the lungs and can become more dangerous when lungs are already irritated.
Why it matters
Dust exposure, crowded living spaces, and delayed care can make infections severe.
Common warning signs
Fever, chills, chest pain, fast breathing, cough with phlegm, or worsening weakness.
What to do early
Seek medical care quickly for high fever, severe breathlessness, or worsening symptoms.

Warning signs

What it means
Symptoms that need urgent attention, especially when they are persistent or worsening.
Why it matters
Early treatment can reduce complications and protect families from preventable loss.
Common warning signs
Coughing blood, severe chest pain, severe breathlessness, high fever, night sweats, and rapid weight loss.
What to do early
Seek urgent medical care. Do not rely on this website to decide whether a condition is serious.

Prevention Guide

Practical steps that protect workers, families, and communities.

01

Reduce dust exposure

Use wet methods where possible, improve ventilation, avoid standing in dust clouds, and separate dusty work from homes and schools.

02

Use appropriate masks or respirators

Cloth alone may not protect against fine dust. Use proper respiratory protection and replace damaged or dirty equipment.

03

Avoid direct mercury exposure

Do not touch mercury with bare hands. Keep it away from food, water, children, and sleeping areas.

04

Do not burn mercury in homes or crowded places

Mercury vapor can harm people nearby, including children and pregnant women.

05

Seek early screening

Persistent cough, chest pain, breathlessness, fever, or unexplained weight loss should be checked by a qualified health professional.

06

Keep children away from mining chemicals

Children are more vulnerable to toxic exposures. Store chemicals safely and keep processing areas separate from family spaces.

07

Report severe symptoms early

Coughing blood, severe chest pain, severe breathlessness, high fever, or rapidly worsening illness needs urgent medical care.

08

Support safer mining practices

Community education, protective equipment, safer processing, health screening, and responsible policy all matter.

Methodology and Sources

Built as a source-backed educational first version.

MineWatch Ghana uses educational risk categories linked to common mining exposures: mineral dust, silica, mercury, processing chemicals, and respiratory illness warning signs. The first version does not collect personal medical data and does not diagnose disease.

Future versions may include verified public datasets, community reports, health-worker partnerships, safer-mining program data, and AI-assisted early risk prediction after ethical review, privacy safeguards, and clinical oversight.
